Wages Accounting for Inflation

Inflation refers to the increase (or decrease) in the cost of goods over time. Inflation is not, by itself, a bad thing. But it is important to ensure that employees are having their wages adjusted - frequently - to account for this natural process.

In the United States of America, and probably other countries around the world, companies rarely follow inflation unless required to by law. This means that inflation could be e.g. 4% but a company might give out 2% pay raises, typically touted as "cost of living adjustments". But if the raise is not at least as much as the inflation rate, then employees are actually losing money.

How it works

Enter your wages (hourly, annual, whatever - anything works) and click "Calculate".

Your results will be presented as a table with each row representing a different month between now and up to 3 years ago. Each row tells you the inflation rate of today when compared to the date stated in that row. If a given row, e.g. Jan 20xx, shows an inflation rate of 5%, then the inflation rate today when compared to the cost of goods in Jan 20xx is 5%. That means that in order to break even on your spending power, you need to be making 5% more than you did in Jan 20xx. The table will also show you what that (adjusted) wage should be at a minimum.

Important!

If you enter your current wages and scroll to the month and year you started earning that much (or to the last row in the results if you've gone that long without a pay increase), you should be making at least the amount specified in that row of the results.

If your current wages are less than the one specified in that row of the results, there is a good possibility that you are actually making less money now than you were when you started earning those wages. Inflation is calculated across all goods purchased, including items such as food and energy. So if, for example, you were making $20/hr a year ago, and inflation between then and now is 5%, then you must make at least $21/hr to maintain your previous lifestyle. If you make less than that, your costs have gone up more than your wages and you are suffering a net loss in terms of spending power.

Disclaimers

This tool currently targets only USA wages. It uses Consumer Price Index (CPI) data from the Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS).

Due to how the BLS API works, this tool can only show data from the present year and the two previous calendar years. That means that e.g. you would only see data as far back as January 2020 for any date you run this tool in all of 2022. It uses calendar years and not a set number of months, so once a new year hits, the oldest year's worth of data is no longer retrievable by the API.
